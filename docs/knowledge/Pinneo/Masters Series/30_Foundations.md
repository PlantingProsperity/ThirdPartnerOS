## 2. PROCEDURAL HEURISTICS (Logic & Algorithms)

### Rule: The "First Guy" Risk Assessment
* **Trigger:** IF you are looking at a development where you are changing the density (e.g., 5 acres to small lots) and you are the first one in the area to do it.
* **Action:** THEN do not be the first developer. Let 2 or 3 others go first. Be the 4th or 5th guy in.
* **Why:** The first developer usually "takes the bullet" (bankruptcy/lawsuits) fighting the neighborhood/city. The subsequent buyers pick up the project at cents on the dollar.

### Rule: The Variance Avoidance Protocol
* **Trigger:** IF a project requires asking for a "Special Use Permit" or a "Variance" (special permission).
* **Action:** THEN assess the neighborhood attitude immediately. If hostile, assume the project will be stalled indefinitely.
* **Strategy:** "Row the boat the direction it’s going." Design within current codes/zoning (By Right) to avoid public appeal processes.
* **Why:** Variances are appealable by the general public; angry neighbors can delay approval for years (see Seattle Condo case).

### Rule: The Fire Hydrant Radius
* **Trigger:** IF designing a short plat (subdivision).
* **Action:** THEN physically measure the distance from the nearest fire hydrant to the *furthest* corner of the furthest proposed lot.
* **The Math:** Distance must be $\le 500$ feet (as the crow flies).
* **Cost Implication:** If $>500$ feet, cost to upsize lines/install hydrant $\approx \$50,000$ (vs. Land Cost of $\approx \$35,000$).

### Rule: Dirt Movement Logistics (Truck Time)
* **Trigger:** IF the site requires Cut and Fill (moving dirt).
* **Action:** THEN calculate cost based on distance to dump site, not just volume.
* **The Math:** $Volume \div Truck Capacity = Total Trips$. (Example: 8,800 yards $\div$ 10-yard truck = 880 trips).
* **Why:** If the dump site is 40 mins away, you are paying for 80 mins of round-trip labor/fuel per load.

## 3. MENTAL MODELS (Philosophy)

* **Principle:** The Wall Urinal Theory
* **Mindset:** "It is what it is." Reality (codes, topography, market conditions) is an independent variable. It will not adjust for your height, urgency, or business plan. You must flex and adapt to it.
* **Application:** Stop expecting the city or the market to be fair ("average"). Expect valid idiosyncrasies and build your plan around them.

* **Principle:** Construction is Real Estate
* **Mindset:** "I am a builder."
* **Application:** You cannot be an effective real estate entrepreneur without a solid working understanding of construction mechanics (soil, utilities, grading).

* **Principle:** Luck vs. Preparation
* **Mindset:** "Luck is like going to Vegas."
* **Application:** Do not rely on being "close enough" (e.g., the 13-foot margin on the fire hydrant). Measure and verify every physical constraint before buying.

## 5. CASE STUDY DATABASE (Structured Data)

* **Case Name:** The Fire Hydrant "Luck"
* **Situation:** Greg was developing a 4-lot short plat. Code required a fire hydrant within 500 feet of every lot corner. He hadn't checked.
* **Strategy Used:** Physically measured with a 250-foot tape.
* **Outcome:** The distance was 487 feet. He had 13 feet to spare. If he had failed, the cost ($50k) would have exceeded the land cost ($35k).
* **Key Lesson:** Don't rely on luck. Infrastructure costs (water mains) can bankrupt a small land deal.

* **Case Name:** The Whooping Crane Flyway
* **Situation:** A friend bought a house zoned Multi-Unit in the city. Intended to develop. Found out the property was in a "Red Zone" (Migratory Flyway).
* **Constraint:** Construction noise disturbs crane reproduction. Building allowed only 3 months/year.
* **Strategy Used:** Prepare everything, then swarm the site during the 3-month window to get the building enclosed (foundation to roof) in 90 days.
* **Key Lesson:** Check Environmental Maps for "Red Zones" before buying.

* **Case Name:** The Seattle Condo Hold-Up
* **Situation:** Developer bought an apartment building to replace with 20 condos. Met all codes/zoning. Did not ask for variances.
* **Obstacle:** Neighbors loved the old building and lobbied against it. Stalled approval for 10 years.
* **Outcome:** Developer went bankrupt due to holding costs. Second buyer failed. Third buyer bought at 30 cents on the dollar and succeeded.
* **Key Lesson:** "Row the boat the direction it's going." Even if legal, neighborhood hostility can kill a project through delay.

* **Case Name:** The CC&R Height Surprise
* **Situation:** Greg started clearing a lot. Neighbor warned him the house looked too tall. Greg cited City Codes.
* **Obstacle:** Neighbor cited CC&Rs (private deed restrictions) which were stricter than city codes. Title company had missed them.
* **Key Lesson:** 99.9% of the time CC&Rs appear on Preliminary Title, but sometimes they don't. You must specifically look for them.
