#### Core Concepts

**1. The Three Approaches to Value**
To effectively buy and structure real estate, an investor must understand how properties are appraised:
*   **Comparative Market Analysis (CMA):** Primarily used for single-family homes. The appraiser sizes up the property (square footage, age, amenities) and compares it to similar properties sold in the neighborhood over the last six months, adjusting for differences.
*   **The Cost Approach:** Often used by insurance companies. It calculates the raw cost of starting over: acquiring the dirt, pulling permits, buying materials (nuts and bolts, lumber, roofing), and paying for labor to rebuild the exact same structure.
*   **The Income Approach:** The absolute most critical valuation method for multi-family and commercial buyers. Value is directly dictated by the revenue the asset can generate. 

**2. The Mechanics of the Income Approach**
The math behind the Income Approach dictates how an investor should evaluate a purchase price. 
*   **Gross Rents:** The total theoretical income generated if the building is fully occupied. (e.g., A 4-plex renting at $1,000/unit/month = $48,000/year).
*   **Net Operating Income (NOI):** Gross Rents minus all operating expenses (Taxes, Insurance, Utilities, Vacancy, Management). Expenses usually run 30% to 35% of Gross Rents. 
*   **Capitalization Rate (Cap Rate):** The rate of return an aggressive investor expects on a secure investment in the current market. 
*   **The Formula:** `NOI ÷ Cap Rate = Property Value`. 
    *   *Note: Cap rates and property values operate inversely. When cap rates go down, property values go up. The best time to sell is when market cap rates are at a world low.*

**3. Expandability (Forcing Appreciation)**
Because the Income Approach ties building value directly to NOI, a buyer can drastically increase the value of a property by increasing its cash flow. In Greg's model, every $100 you improve the monthly cash flow increases the building's value by approximately $20,000. Do not buy a property based on *potential* future rents ("giving away your entrepreneurship"); buy based on the *proven, current* 6-to-8 month rent roll, and then apply Expandability to create explosive equity.

**4. Evaluating Expenses Rigorously**
Before making an offer, you must realistic calculate your expenses to protect your cash flow ("You eat cash flow, you don't eat equity"):
*   **Principal & Interest:** The negotiated monthly debt service.
*   **Property Tax:** Historically, this always trends upward.
*   **Insurance:** The most volatile expense. Do not assume you will inherit the seller's low premium, especially on older brick buildings.
*   **Utilities:** A massive expense item. Account for who pays water, sewer, garbage, and heat (radiant hot water systems).
*   **Vacancy Factor:** Look at the micro-economic climate. A 15% vacancy rate severely changes the NOI.
*   **Management Expense:** Even if you manage it yourself, calculate a management fee. Your time is valuable, and the property must be able to afford a manager if you step away.

#### The Toolbox

*   **"And/or Assigns":** When writing the buyer's name on the Purchase and Sale Agreement, always include "and/or assigns." This gives you the flexibility to pass the deal to a third party for a fee or bring on a partner during escrow.
*   **The Blank Addendum:** Do not rely solely on standard forms. Create custom addendums ("Maker and Beneficiary agree as follows...") to control the terms of the Promissory Note and the Deed of Trust.
*   **First Right of Refusal:** A clause placed in the Promissory Note stating: *"Maker of said note has first right of refusal to purchase note should beneficiary elect to sell note prior to maturity."* This allows you to buy your own debt at a discount if the seller needs cash.
*   **Co-Beneficiaries:** If doing seller financing with an elderly seller, name their co-beneficiaries directly on the note to clearly dictate who receives payments if the primary beneficiary passes away.
*   **Substitution of Security:** A highly strategic clause allowing the buyer to move the collateral securing the Promissory Note from the subject property to a different property. It requires the buyer to provide written notice, an appraisal on the new collateral, and a title report proving sufficient equity. *This must be negotiated upfront, not five years down the road.*
*   **Strategic Contingencies:** 
    *   *Inspection Contingency:* To review the structural integrity of the asset.
    *   *Review of Rental Agreements:* To ensure you aren't inheriting "sweetheart deals" (e.g., a 20-year lease at $100/month written by the seller for a family member).
    *   *Review of Tax Returns:* To verify the *actual* collected rents and expenses, not just the advertised proforma.

#### Case Studies

**The 38-Unit on Queen Anne**
Greg purchased a 38-unit apartment building in the Queen Anne neighborhood. 
*   **The Acquisition:** The building was generating just under $11,000 per month in gross rents. Using the Income Approach, Greg determined the value and purchased it for $1.2 million.
*   **The Expandability Application:** Immediately after closing, Greg implemented a massive rehab strategy. All tenants moved out, all 38 units were renovated, and the units were re-rented at market rate.
*   **The Result:** The new rent roll jumped to $18,750 per month (an increase of roughly $8,000 per month). Because value is directly tied to income, this $8,000/month boost in cash flow created a massive, immediate leap in the overall value of the property.

#### Action Items

*   **Analyze the Market:** Understand the current standard Capitalization Rate in your target location.
*   **Draft Your Paperwork:** Obtain or draft a custom blank addendum, Promissory Note, and Deed of Trust so you can dictate terms like *Right of Refusal* and *Substitution of Security*.
*   **Write Specific Contingencies:** Never buy an income property without writing in contingencies for "Review of Rental Agreements" and "Review of Tax Returns."
*   **Manage the Handoff:** Do not accept a coffee can full of unmarked keys at closing. Demand a physical meeting with the seller at the property to tag every key to its specific unit and discuss the building's physical systems.
*   **Establish Tenant Relations:** Send an introduction letter immediately post-closing. Inform inherited tenants where to send rent, outline your management expectations, and confirm the status of their security deposits.
*   **Get in Motion:** Write down exactly what you want, back up to your current position, and take the very first step. "Success is simply trying."
