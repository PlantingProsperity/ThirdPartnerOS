Mastering The “Bird Letter” 

 
 
 
 
 
 
Bird Letter Check List 

Identify your Target Area 

If looking for investment property, this should be relatively close to where 
you live so that you can be involved, hands on, and aware of the property, its 
condition, the tenants, etc.  And if any of you have listened to Greg for any 
length of time you know that it is of nonnegotiable importance that your 
“Target” areas are high demand locations.  And within these areas you will 
make it your business to be well versed on values, rental and sales rates on a 
per square foot basis, sales comps, zoning, etc.  In other words, find a high 
demand area within reasonable distance to where you live and make it your 
business to know absolutely everything you need to know about your chosen 
stomping grounds. 

Obtain lists from Title Company 

In this area we use title companies to get the information we need, but your 
source can vary depending on where you live.  You may get the information 
from the county or a private public record source but regardless of who you 
go to, the information you need to obtain remains the same.  Once you have 
this data in your hot little hands this is what constitutes your Farm Package.  
Your best bet is to get a detailed street map of your area.  Highlight different 
Thomas Guide areas that you want to mail to and when, and then ask the 
title company to send you those packages at regular intervals.  Keep in mind 
that you want the package the month BEFORE you mail to that area.  So if 
you mail to Area 500 every June and January make sure the title company 
gets you that list every May and December.  By the way, I hope it goes 
without saying that you shouldn’t mail Bird Letters in December.  Not only 
will your efforts get lost amidst the shuffle of Christmas cards and Holiday 
well wishes, but I can only hope that you yourself will be spending this time 
in ways that put the focus on things more Merry and Bright.   
When requesting your Farm Package, ask for ALL multi-family properties in 
your area as well as all single family homes in the lower 1/3 of tax assessed 
value.  In other words, if the homes in Area 500 range in price from 

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
$100,000 to $900,000 you are looking at an $800,000 spread.  When you 
divide 800 by 3 you get approximately $266K.  Keep it simple and round off 
to $250,000 and add that to your low end tax assessed value of $100,000.  
So now you want a farm package for all single family homes tax assessed at 
$350,000 or less in addition to all the multiplexes.  You could request 
additional parameters from the title company if you would like such as 
selecting for non-owner occupied homes only or out of state owners only.  
The point being that you can tailor make these Farm Packages to suit your 
own personal goals and objectives. 

In the past we have also requested that the list include only homes where 
there has been no sale or transfer for the last 10 years.  But at this point in 
my experience of never knowing what scenario may present itself, combined 
with an extremely unusual housing market, I would say that this is a 
parameter that is largely obsolete in the here and now.   

We have included a sample map for you to have an idea of how to break 
down the areas and mail to them accordingly.  How many areas and who you 
choose to mail to depends entirely on what exactly it is you are trying to 
accomplish so the included map is simply meant as a point of reference. 

BIG stamps 

As Greg says, we are looking for the large picture stamps.  They have to 
come on a sheet, not a roll.  That means, No Liberty Bell stamps, no Flag 
stamps, no “Forever” stamps and definitely do not EVER use a meter to 
stamp your bird letters.  If you mess with this rule I can promise that you 
will have just wasted a good deal of money on postage and your letter will 
be filling recycling bins everywhere. 

Envelopes 

Over the years we have studied the envelope question to a degree that could 
nearly seem ridiculous.  But plenty of trial and error has led us to an 
awareness of the Gold Standard in this department.  A colored 5x7 envelope.  
Our own little theory of relativity as it were.  The envelope you choose is 
HIGHLY relevant to the rate at which people choose to open and read your 
impressive handiwork.  Format your letter so that your copy center can 
easily and neatly cut them to fit nicely into that most important 5x7 space.   

 
 
 
 
 
 
DO NOT pull out a pair of scissors and cut your letters down to size.  I 
would also recommend against folding an 8 ½ by 11 page to fit because no 
matter how you try to do it, it’s going to be awkward and unimpressive.  If 
you absolutely must use a standard size envelope just make sure it isn’t 
white. 

Printer paper 

I’ve had people use really nice card stock stationary when mailing to a small 
area where they were trying to find a home for themselves which was a 
really nice touch, but can get fairly pricey.  Money well spent for a small 
target area when looking for a personal residence.  Not absolutely necessary 
when doing more of a “mass mailing,” but the heavier the stock the better.  
To be truthful, one of the best Bird Letter responses we have ever received 
was when we had the “letter” printed onto a folded card.  So if you can 
swing the cost go for it.  But if you aren’t doing card stock, a good rule of 
thumb is to always use paper that is at least 24 lb. minimum weight and 
again, off-white or colored is best. 

Font and Size 

Be sure to select a font that looks business like and professional.  This 
shouldn’t look like an invitation to the local Renaissance Fair.  So take your 
pick but make sure it is easy to read and business like.  Try to go for a 
somewhat larger font size and know that too small a font will discourage 
people from actually reading your letter.  Twelve is the minimum and I 
would recommend a 14 point font.   

Labor to hand address envelopes if you aren’t doing it 
yourself 

If you are sending out any number of these I highly recommend you find 
someone to address your bird letters.  Great groups of people that are often 
up for the job are high school kids, retired friends or family or anybody you 
know that has available time and really nice penmanship.  Having had a 

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
relative in a retirement home (not a nursing home) I can tell you that MANY 
of the residents would love to have something to do besides Bingo.   

Agent/Broker Disclosure Language 

This is a bit of a grey area as different states have different requirements, but 
none of them have a clear directive.  If you are a broker or agent you must 
disclose that fact.  The grey area surrounds exactly when that disclosure 
needs to be made.  I have always been of the mind that it is better to be 
completely transparent and upfront.  You can, and should, still state that 
while you are, in fact, an agent/broker, this is a personal pursuit and you are 
not looking for a listing.  So find the language that suits you.  Make it clear 
and concise but don’t allow it to occupy more than 2-3 lines of your letter. 

“Editor” to review your letters and offer honest 
feedback 

Grammar can be tricky and spell check can only do so much.  ALWAYS 
have a second set of eyes take a look at your letter to give you feedback.  For 
men writing letters I always suggest they have their wife, mother or female 
friend take a look at the letter.  With all due respect, men’s letters can 
sometimes lack the finesse that will get the phone to ring so make good use 
of the softer side when reviewing your letter.  The objective is always to put 
yourself in the space of the person receiving and reading your letter and I 
have just found that generally speaking, women are better at this.   
I would also encourage you to be candid with yourself about your own 
strengths and weaknesses when drafting your letters.  Personally, I have a 
tendency to get too wordy and to over write for my audience.  I have to 
constantly remind myself of who I am writing to and adjust accordingly.   
Having said that, I believe it is extremely important that the letter sounds 
like the person that they will eventually meet.  So don’t have your friend that 
teaches English Literature at the University create your letter for you.  
Consider it a cautionary tale that you don’t want to send out a letter that 
seems as though Shakespeare himself has penned it only to show up thinking 
that when the seller mentions the “Taming of the Shrew” he is referring to 
an ex-wife.  People have an amazing radar for disingenuousness so I would 

 
 
 
 
 
 
strongly recommend against having someone else do your homework for 
you. 

Software 

Make sure you have some sort of software that can do a mail merge.  You 
need to have a system whereby you can write the letter and then have your 
mail merge drop in the names and address you have received from the Title 
Company.  We of course live in the land of Microsoft, but whether you are a 
Mac or a PC make sure you have a program that is easy and comfortable to 
use.  While this part of your process needs to be automated DO NOT print 
address labels to put on your envelopes.  This always needs to be done by 
hand.   

Structure of the Bird Letter 1-2-3 

1.  The first paragraph of your bird letter should tell the reader something 
about you.  You are going to be creating something of much greater 
value and much more readability if you include some familiar and 
relevant information about yourself.  This will add much needed 
warmth and sincerity to a business request.   
This is never the place to tell them what you want; it is the place to 
tell them who you are.  To be honest I am constantly amazed at how 
often this is done.  I think that when writing bird letters people can be 
incredibly eager to get the ball rolling but the best way to do that is 
going to be with a lot of tact and grace.  So start softly, tell them a bit 
about yourself and why you are contacting them.  Be warm, but not 
too personal.  Be as specific as you can be in terms of mentioning the 
area their home is in and why you are drawn to their specific 
neighborhood. In short, warmth, a few specific details if possible and 
a very brief impression of who you are is all the first paragraph should 
consist of.   

2.  The second paragraph is where you want to lay out your aims and 

intents in a very unassuming manner.  Let them know of your interest 
and what you can offer in terms of skills and expertise.  You need to 
put yourself out there as a hard working problem solver and let them 

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
know what you are about and how you do business.  This is where 
you need to find just the right mix of the personal and professional.  A 
bit like the perfect mimosa.  It needs just the right parts orange juice 
and champagne.  Too much orange juice and it’s too sweet.  Too 
much champagne and it’s overpowering.  Likewise, if you have too 
much fluff your letter is too sweet and they aren’t going to take you 
seriously.  If you come on too strong they will feel overpowered and 
therefore wary and suspicious of your intentions. So the second 
paragraph is all about mixing the perfect mimosa.   

*If you are an agent the second paragraph is where I would incorporate your 
disclosure language.  You don’t want to close your letter with a disclaimer 
that sounds like a complicated bit of legalese.  Find a way to incorporate it 
matter of factly into the second paragraph. 

The final portion of the letter is little more than a couple 
3. 
sentences to thank them sincerely for their time and to let them know 
how best to reach you.  I think phone numbers and email addresses are 
enough points of contact and will certainly suffice.  Adding links to 
Facebook pages and Twitter feeds is just plain over the top and it will 
alienate much of your client base.  Don’t do it. 

Last but not least sign your name.  This should be done by hand.  If you are 
sending out large batches of Bird Letters, pay the little bit of extra money to 
print your letters in black and your signature in blue.  It isn’t on par with 
signing your letters by hand, but in volume, it can be the only reasonable 
approach.  It remains important even when hand addressing letters that your 
letter is printed in black, and you sign your name with a blue pen.  At that 
point, it stands out as something that you have truly given your personal 
time an attention to.   

I want to add a bit of a footnote here that needs to be true for your bird 
letters and I would hope for your life in general.  And that is to do what you 
say you are going to do.  As Dr. Seuss would say, “simple it sounds, but I’m 
afraid it is not.”  If you say you can custom craft a deal that meets a sellers 
needs and objectives, well you better be educated enough to do just that.  If 
you say you are comfortable buying homes in an “as is” condition, you 
better be really comfortable with a nailing belt on or have reasonably priced 
subcontractors that you trust at the ready.  If you put it out there that you can 

 
 
 
 
pay “All Cash!” for properties, make sure you have your piggy bank handy.  
You get the idea. 

Email Address 

As I have just made mention of including your phone number and your 
email address, make sure that your email address is entirely appropriate for 
the type of business you are doing.  If you want to buy homes, don’t have an 
email address that reads brian@ibuycheaphouses.com.  Make it something 
very neutral and unassuming because that mistake alone can make the 
difference between them picking up the phone or throwing away your letter.   

Length of the Letter 

A Bird Letter should never be more than a single page.  Generally speaking 
it is okay for Personal Bird letters to be a bit longer as you do want to go 
into a bit more detail about yourself when looking for a home to live in 
rather than just invest in.  But with all your letters, if they are too long 
nobody will take the time to read them.  Having said that, if they are too 
short, I can guarantee you haven’t done your job well enough and you need 
to put a bit more effort into your message.   

Pictures 

Something I get asked about a lot is including a picture, or pictures on your 
Bird Letter.  To be perfectly honest, generally speaking, I am not a fan.  But 
I should also add that I’m not fond of pictures on business cards or 
letterhead.  So I fully acknowledge that this is just a personal preference on 
my part.  So while I am, as a rule, not a fan of pictures on bird letters, I’ve 
seen them used in a manner that works for the situation at hand.  If you have 
a growing family, or a new baby and choose to include a family picture this 
can be entirely appropriate, (but never more than one).  However, if you’re 
30 years old and building up your real estate portfolio, don’t include a 
picture of you catching air while snowboarding in Aspen.  Once again, you 
get the idea…use good judgment.   

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Having said that, DO NOT include pictures when mailing to your multi-
plexes.  In this instance you are dealing with an entirely different type of 
seller and with this particular letter, expertise and professionalism must lead 
the way. So when writing your multi-plex Bird Letter, or any like it, your 
family photo should stay on your desktop.   

The Double Standard is alive and well… 

Regardless of our opinion on this topic, the reality remains the same.  
Double standards can and do exist and whether or not you choose to 
personally wage war on them is not what we need to concern ourselves with 
when it comes to Bird Letters.  But when it comes to writing our Bird 
Letters, we need to allow for the reality that they do, in fact, exist.   If you 
are a woman writing bird letters, you need to emphasize your expertise and 
professionalism.  For guys, you often need to soften your demeanor and 
present yourself in a conscientious and trustworthy manner.   

So are stereotypes… 

I am often reminded of the fact that many people have negative connotations 
around the word “investor.”  This is something I need to consistently force 
myself to be aware of as I have no preconceived negative notions of it 
myself.  But apparently enough people do harbor feelings of ill-will toward 
the word, (or more correctly, what it means to them) that it is a word we 
need to be very careful with.  So, in its place, lean toward using words like 
investments and investing.  For some borderline unexplainable reason, these 
words don’t pack the same unpleasant impact.  Age can also be a stereotype 
you are forced to contend with.  If the owner of 15 apartment buildings in 
the area gets your letter and you look like you would be ID’ d every time 
you try to buy a beer, they may dismiss you out of hand as inexperience and 
unprofessional.  So really think it through and be very measured in the 
choices you make.   

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
To Logo or not to Logo… 

I really feel it is important, whether you are a full time real estate investor or 
somebody who is tackling it on a very part-time basis, to create a logo for 
yourself and your business.  While I mentioned before that we want to steer 
clear of presenting ourselves as professional real estate investors, we need to 
present ourselves as professionals nonetheless.  No need to hire a graphic 
artist or some high powered firm to tell you that the word “Starbucks” 
should no longer be part of your presence.  There are an infinite number of 
ways to design something that suits you and your business without any cost.  
Keep in mind that you are walking a fine line here.  Your logo needs to be 
appealing without being overly business-like. (Remember our discussion 
about people’s impression of professional real estate investors?)  Work to 
create something original and imaginative.  Something pleasing, appealing 
and perhaps with the ability to make them just a little bit curious.  Once 
you’ve created your masterpiece this too I would encourage you to put in 
front of several people who will give you their HONEST feedback.  Does it 
convey the image and impression you are hoping it would?  Do the shapes 
and colors draw and interest people or put them off?  This is actually an 
astonishingly scientific study, but creating something you like and an image 
people will respond to favorably is a great starting point.  
*One important caveat would be that you NOT include your business logo 
on a Bird Letter that you are writing for your own accord.  In other words, if 
you are writing a letter looking for a personal residence for yourself or your 
family, leave the logo off.   

Know your Audience 

As you are drafting your Bird Letters be very mindful of your general 
audience.  Are there certain givens or consistencies you should be 
consciensous about?  For example, if you are mailing to an area that is on 
the cusp of regeneration and you have a large population of elderly 
homeowners, be especially sensitive to any specific set of needs they may 
have.  If the community you are mailing to has a large ethnic population 
keep your language simple and straightforward and avoid cliché’s and 
phrases that may mean something to you, but will baffle them.  If you are 
mailing to owner occupied homes don’t use the word HOUSE, to refer to the 
HOME they have lovingly tended to and raised their family in.  The two 

 
 
 
 
words don’t evoke anywhere near the same feeling in the reader.  I see this 
as being just basic awareness and courtesy to your audience and without it 
you may as well be yelling in church.  People aren’t going to hear a word 
you say and you will likely even be offending them.   

Voicemail 

Now that you have gone through all this impressive effort and energy to 
craft the perfect Bird Letter the entire aim is to get the phone to ring.  And 
when it does, you had better be prepared.  While this obviously isn’t part of 
a Bird Letter it is an utterly essential ingredient nonetheless.  We strongly 
encourage people to get a separate number that they use only for Bird 
Letters.  There is no need to buy another phone and it can easily be set up so 
that phone number rings on your phone simply with a different tone so as to 
identify it.  And attached to that number needs to be a concise, intelligent, 
voicemail specifically geared to those who respond to your letters.  “Yo 
Dude,” is not how your voicemail should begin.  And I don’t want to hear 
kids in the background or be in any way confused about whom I am calling 
and what is expected of me and what I can expect of you. 

5 Types of Bird Letters 

Around our office we employ five general categories of Bird Letters. 

They are: 

Personal Use 
Single Family Owner Occupied   (I, II, III) 
Single Family Non-Owner Occupied  
Multi-Family 
Specific Issue Property 

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
I have included examples of each and I need to stress with every fiber of my 
being that these are, in fact EXAMPLES.  They are not templates for your 
letters and should never be used as such.  Bird Letters absolutely must be 
personal, specific, and regionally relatable.  The “Personal Use” Bird Letter 
I have included was not written by me, but as I consider it to be one of the 
best I have ever read, I wanted to include this example.  The Personal Use 
letters I have written in the past have always received, by a long shot, the 
greatest response rate of all the Bird Letters we mail.  This is primarily 
because this market is solicited a bit less combined with the fact that the 
personal nature of the letter encourages a higher response rate.  So take your 
time on this one as it can be the most important and impacting of the letters 
you write and often opens many unexpected doors. 

If you are going to mail numerous times to specific areas, you should have a 
progression of letters that you mail out so that the recipients feel less like 
part of a production line and more like people you are genuinely interested 
in.  I have included one example of such a progression.  While around here 
we have mailings every month, each target area gets approximately 3 letters 
a year from us.  So I have included a progression of letters for a Single 
Family Owner Occupied mailing to give you a good idea of the look and feel 
your follow-on letters should have.   

To be sure, we could certainly come up with a more specific breakdown of 
categories and if you are so inclined please feel free to create as many letters 
as you feel you need to properly address your audience.  For example, I 
could create a Single Family Owner Occupied Letter that is specifically for 
the Queen Anne neighborhood of Seattle.  Or, if I was specifically directing 
my efforts toward out of state owners I would definitely write a separate 
letter for that group.  So these are the general categories and feel free to 
create others as your time and creativity allow. 

I have also included quite a few “Before and After” letters as I find and 
consider this to be an incredibly effective way to learn.  As you will see, 
some of the letters bear little resemblance to the original.  Blade jobs as Greg 
would say.  Others reflect much more minor and or subtle changes so I think 
it is valuable to see and glean something from both.   

It is important to know that by no means have we created the end all, be all, 
definitive guide to writing Bird Letters.  But what I DO know is that you’ll 
be off to a darn good start.  Composing Bird Letters is much like a favorite 

 
 
 
 
recipe.  As long as you follow some basic and fundamental principles, you 
can add a little of this and a little of that to suit your personal taste.  As well, 
the flavor or your Bird Letter should be relevant to whom you are and the 
sort of business you are looking to build.  Obviously we have never taken 
the “We Buy Houses – All Cash” approach that is a bit akin to yelling at 
people in writing, but this is certainly an approach that exists.  As well as an 
approach that would require a very different sort of letter.  So while there is 
room for everything we certainly have our occasional opinions.  But here 
and now we have laid out some essentials.  Rest assured it has been hard 
won over many years of trial and error.  And I can only hope that a bit of 
what we have learned can be of great help to you and your business.  These 
aren’t secrets, just sound principles to get you started in the right direction or 
add horse power to what you are already doing and achieving. 

It is our hope and intention that you find this material of value as that 
remains our highest and greatest concern.  We look forward to reading the 
results of your efforts and ever and always wish you the very best in all your 
endeavors.
